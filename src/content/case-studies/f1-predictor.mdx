## The Challenge

Predicting an F1 winner is one of the most complex problems in sports analytics. Beyond driver skill, you must account for tire strategy, real-time telemetry, safety cars, and the massive regulatory reset of 2026.

I built a system that combines three fundamentally different modeling philosophies to capture the full nuance of a race weekend.

## The Triple-Model Architecture

### 1. XGBoost: The Pattern Finder
Trained on **1,200+ historical races** (2014-2025). It learns non-linear relationships like the "grid position decay"—how P1 to P2 loss of win probability is far more severe than P10 to P11.
- handles severe class imbalance (1 winner vs 19 non-winners) via `scale_pos_weight`.

### 2. Monte Carlo: The Physics Engine
Instead of patterns, this models the *process*. It runs 10,000 parallel-universe race simulations, sampling:
- **Lap-to-lap variability** (Normal distribution)
- **Tire Degradation** (Constructor-specific rates)
- **Safety Car Luck** (1% per-lap probability at Albert Park)
- **DNF Risks** (Elevated for the 2026 reg-change year)

### 3. Bayesian Inference: The Belief Updater
Starts with "Priors" (historical driver dominance) and updates them with qualifying "Evidence". It handles the "Unknown Unknowns" of 2026 by widening confidence intervals when uncertainty is high.

---

## Technical Edge: 2026 Wildcards

Most models failed because they lacked 2026 context. I engineered specific features for:
- **Active Aero Era**: Modeling dynamic front/rear wing adjustments.
- **Energy Management**: The 50/50 ICE-to-Electric power split.
- **Reliability Risk**: Applied a multiplier for backmarker teams running unproven power units (Cadillac, Audi).

## Results

The ensemble accurately predicted a 38.9% win probability for Russell in the Australian GP, correctly identifying that while his pace was dominant, the reliability risk and energy management gaps made it far from a "sure thing."

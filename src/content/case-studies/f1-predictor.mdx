## The Abstract
Predicting a Formula 1 race winner is one of the most complex problems in sports analytics. Unlike high-frequency sports, F1 offers a tiny sample size (22-24 races per year) with enormous confounding variables: atmospheric conditions, tire degradation, safety car probability, and mechanical reliability. 

With the massive **2026 Regulatory Reset**, historical data became partially obsolete. This system was designed to bridge that gap using a triple-model ensemble that balances historical patterns with real-time physical simulations.

### Tier 1: High Predictive Power
- **Grid Position**: Historically explains ~45% of race variance. Modeled as a non-linear decay (the P1 advantage is structurally different from P10).
- **Constructor Strength**: A rolling window of constructor points acting as a proxy for raw car performance.
- **Qualifying Pace Delta**: The absolute gap in seconds to the pole sitter, capturing the gradient of competitiveness.

## 🧠 The Triple-Model Ensemble

### Model 1: XGBoost Classifier (Pattern Matching)
Uses gradient boosting on 12 years of hybrid-era data (2014-2025). It handles the severe class imbalance (1 winner per 20 entries) using `scale_pos_weight` to penalize misclassifying a winner 20x more heavily.

```python
# Handling class imbalance in F1 data (1 winner vs 19 losers)
scale_pos = n_neg_historical / n_pos_historical

self.model = XGBClassifier(
    max_depth=5,
    learning_rate=0.1,
    n_estimators=150,
    subsample=0.8,
    scale_pos_weight=scale_pos,
    eval_metric="logloss",
    random_state=42
)
```

### Model 2: Monte Carlo Simulator (Physics Engine)
Models the race lap-by-lap, 10,000 times. For each simulation, it samples from a Normal distribution for lap times, applies linear tire degradation, and "rolls for luck" on safety cars and mechanical failures.

```python
# The Physics Loop: Simulating a single race lap
for lap in range(1, self.n_laps + 1):
    for d in drivers:
        # 1. Check for mechanical DNF
        if self.rng.random() < d.dnf_prob_per_lap:
            d.is_active = False
            continue
            
        # 2. Apply Tire Degradation
        tire_penalty = d.tire_deg_rate * d.current_tire_age
        
        # 3. Calculate Lap Time (Physics + Driver Consistency)
        lap_time = d.base_lap_time + tire_penalty + self.rng.normal(0, d.lap_time_sigma)
        d.total_time += lap_time
```

### Model 3: Bayesian Inference (The Belief Updater)
Starts with "Priors" based on historical driver dominance and updates with "Evidence" from the current qualifying weekend. It explicitly quantifies the uncertainty the other models handle implicitly.

## 🔍 Key Engineering Insights
1. **Grid Position Dominance**: Pole position wins 57% of races at Albert Park historically. Russell's P1 qualifying gives him a massive structural advantage.
2. **Safety Car Wildcard**: Safety car probability at Albert Park is **~60%**. A safety car would compress the field and give Verstappen (starting from P20) a realistic recovery path.
3. **Energy Management Era**: The 50/50 ICE-to-Electric split is brand new. Teams that misjudge battery deployment could lose significant pace in the final stint.

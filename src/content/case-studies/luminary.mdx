## The Problem With Music Apps

Every major music streaming platform uses the same core recommendation loop: listen history → collaborative filtering → personalized playlist. It's a well-solved problem, and it works. But it has a fundamental constraint: it models who you were, not who you are right now.

Your mood at 7am on a Tuesday is not your mood at 11pm on a Friday. Your focus state during deep work is not your state during a post-run cool-down. History-based recommendation systems miss this entirely because they have no access to present-tense emotional state.

The hypothesis behind Luminary: what if the interface could read the room?

---

## Research: 24 Interviews, Three Patterns

Before writing any code I spent three weeks interviewing 24 people about their relationship with music and emotional state. The protocol was semi-structured — I wanted to understand the decision points, not just the preferences.

Three patterns emerged consistently:

**Context-switching is exhausting.** People described the cognitive load of searching for the right playlist when their mood had shifted. "I just want the app to know" came up in 18 of 24 interviews. The friction wasn't about not knowing what they wanted — it was about having to articulate and search for it.

**Discovery fatigue is real.** Algorithmic queues that auto-play indefinitely created paralysis. "There's too much choice" sounds paradoxical for a problem about music, but the data was clear: people preferred a curated, short, emotionally-matched selection over an infinite generative stream.

**Emotional resonance beats audio quality.** Participants consistently preferred music that matched their mood over objectively higher-quality recordings that didn't. A lo-fi track that fit the moment beat a studio master that didn't. This has obvious implications for how we weight signals in the recommendation system.

## Layer 1: Affect Detection

The emotion detection layer runs entirely in the browser using **TensorFlow.js** with a MobileNetV2 backbone, fine-tuned on the AffectNet and FER+ datasets for seven discrete emotional states: happy, calm, focused, sad, anxious, excited, neutral.

Why MobileNet specifically? Three reasons. First, it's designed for inference on constrained hardware — a browser running WebGL-accelerated TensorFlow doesn't have the same compute budget as a server-side model. Second, the depthwise-separable convolutions in MobileNet naturally discard high-frequency spatial detail, which matters for affect recognition: we care about macro facial expressions, not skin texture. Third, the architecture is small enough that the model file ships with the initial page load at around 14MB, keeping the initial permission-grant experience snappy.

The model outputs a probability distribution over the seven states, sampled at 30fps. Raw per-frame probabilities would produce chaotic, jittering state changes — I needed a smoothing mechanism.

---

## The 8-Second Smoothing Insight

The first version of Luminary was genuinely unpleasant to use. The emotional state changed too quickly. If you shifted your expression slightly, the audio would shift slightly, and the UI would shift slightly, and you'd feel watched rather than understood. Users in usability testing described it as "anxious" — the app's response to them was making them more anxious, not less.

The fix was an 8-second exponential moving average over the state probability distribution. Transitions only trigger when a new state maintains above-threshold probability for 8 sustained seconds. This means:

- Passing expressions don't change the experience
- The app feels like it's reading your deeper state, not your face
- The audio transitions feel like natural weather changes rather than hard cuts

Getting to 8 seconds took 11 iterations of the permission and transition flow in usability testing. Too short (2-3 seconds) felt reactive and anxious. Too long (15+ seconds) felt unresponsive. 8 seconds matched how users described noticing their own mood shifts.

## Layer 2: Generative Audio

Each emotional state maps to a generative audio graph built with the **Web Audio API**. The system synthesizes ambient texture in real-time rather than playing back pre-recorded tracks.

The graph structure for each state:
- **Oscillator network:** 3-4 sine wave oscillators at harmonically related frequencies, modulated by low-frequency oscillators (LFOs) for gentle movement
- **Filtered noise:** White noise passed through bandpass filters tuned to the emotional register (high-frequency for excited/anxious, low-frequency for calm/sad)
- **Pad layer:** Sampled chord textures convolved with reverb impulse responses for spatial depth
- **Dynamics:** A master compressor that holds perceived loudness constant as state transitions occur

Transitions between states use linear gain crossfades over 8-12 seconds, triggered by the state smoother. The result feels like gradual weather changing rather than switching playlists.

The generative approach was a deliberate choice over curated playlists. With pre-recorded tracks, transitions require sync points — you have to wait for a track to end, or accept an abrupt cut. With generated audio, transitions can happen continuously at any moment.

## Layer 3: Adaptive Interface

Beyond audio, the visual interface responds to detected state across four dimensions:

**Color temperature** shifts between warm (excited, happy) and cool (calm, focused, sad) using CSS custom property interpolation.

**Animation speed** scales from near-static (focused, calm) to more fluid and dynamic (excited, happy). This is implemented via a `--animation-rate` CSS variable that all motion declarations reference.

**Typography weight** increases with emotional intensity — calm and neutral states use lighter weights, which reduces visual density and cognitive load.

**Layout density** collapses secondary information when focused state is detected (reducing distraction) and expands to show more when the user is in an exploratory state.

## The Trust Problem

The hardest engineering challenge wasn't the ML model. It was designing the permission flow.

Asking for webcam access for a music app is asking for something significant. Users needed to feel the product was respecting their privacy before they'd grant camera permission. My early designs failed because they asked for permission too early (before any value demonstration) or explained privacy too technically (people don't read permission dialogs).

The solution after 11 iterations: show the app working — visually, compellingly — before asking for camera access at all. The first 30 seconds show an animated simulation of emotional state detection and soundscape adaptation using a stock face. Only after the user has experienced the product concept do we ask for permission, and we do it with a single sentence: "Luminary uses your camera to detect your mood. All analysis happens on your device — nothing is sent or stored."

That combination — demonstrate value first, ask second, be one sentence about privacy — moved the camera grant rate from 41% to 84% in A/B testing.

## Results

After a 6-week beta with 340 users:

- **+340%** average session length vs control group using Spotify
- **78%** of users said it "felt like the app understood them"
- **92%** 30-day retention rate (vs ~30% industry average for music apps)
- **84%** camera permission grant rate after final permission flow design

The session length result was the most surprising. We expected improvement — that was the hypothesis. But 340% was dramatically higher than the 40-60% lift I'd projected based on comparable adaptive interface research. Post-study interviews suggested the generative audio played a significant role: users weren't just listening longer, they were entering deeper focus states because the audio environment was continuously reinforced rather than interrupted by track transitions.

## Stack

- **Framework:** Next.js 14 (App Router) + TypeScript
- **ML Inference:** TensorFlow.js 4.x + fine-tuned MobileNetV2
- **Audio:** Web Audio API (generative synthesis, no pre-recorded samples)
- **Animation:** Framer Motion + CSS custom properties
- **State Management:** Zustand
- **Deployment:** Vercel Edge Functions
- **Training data:** AffectNet, FER+, custom annotation set (n=2,400 frames)

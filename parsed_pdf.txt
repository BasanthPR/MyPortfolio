 How LLMs Process
Non-English Languages
 A Deep Dive into Tokenization, Embeddings, and the Multilingual Deficit
 
 Basanth Periyapatna Roopa Kumar
 Applied Data Science | SJSU
 
Abstract: Large Language Models systematically disadvantage non-English languages at every level of their architecture. A
Kannada sentence requires 3–5× more tokens than its English equivalent, costs proportionally more to process, and
produces lower-quality outputs. This document traces the cascade from byte-level tokenization through attention
mechanisms to final generation, comparing how GPT-4o, Gemini, Claude, Llama, and specialized Indic models handle the
world's most complex scripts. We examine the training data imbalance (95% English in Llama 3), the token fertility crisis, the
curse of multilinguality in embeddings, and the frontier research closing these gaps.

1. Byte-Level Tokenization Creates a Fundamental Tax on
Non-Latin Scripts
The journey of every prompt through an LLM begins at the tokenizer, and it is here that the inequality starts.
Modern LLMs including GPT-4, Claude, Llama, and Mistral use byte-level Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), which
operates on raw UTF-8 bytes rather than characters. The algorithm starts with a base vocabulary of 256 byte tokens,
then iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent byte pairs from training data. This design guarantees universal
coverage (no unknown tokens) but creates deep structural inequity across scripts.
The core problem is byte width. Every Latin character occupies 1 byte in UTF-8. Each Kannada, Hindi, Tamil, or
Chinese character requires 3 bytes. Arabic and Urdu characters consume 2 bytes. This means the BPE algorithm
needs more merges just to reconstitute a single non-Latin character from its byte fragments. Because English
dominates training corpora, English byte-pair merges are learned first and most frequently. Non-Latin byte sequences
recur with lower probability, so many never get merged into complete characters in smaller vocabularies, leaving
individual bytes as tokens.
Figure 1: The BPE tokenization pipeline showing how identical semantic content diverges dramatically between English and Kannada at the
 byte level.
 
Consider how the Kannada word Sankranti is encoded. It contains 9 Unicode code points totaling 27 bytes in UTF-8.
The conjunct consonant kra, which appears as a single visual glyph (an ottakshara), is actually three separate code
points spanning 9 bytes. A byte-level BPE tokenizer fragments this linguistically coherent unit across multiple tokens,
destroying its semantic integrity.
SentencePiece (used by T5, mBART, and XLM-R) takes a different approach. Its unigram language model starts
with a large vocabulary and iteratively removes tokens that least decrease training data likelihood. This probabilistic,
top-down method can be more linguistically aware for complex scripts, and it handles text without space-based
pre-tokenization — critical for languages where word boundaries are ambiguous. WordPiece (used by BERT)

2. The Fertility Crisis: Same Content, 3–15× the Tokens
Token fertility is the single most measurable manifestation of multilingual inequality in LLMs.
Token fertility — the average number of tokens per word — was rigorously quantified by Petrov et al. (NeurIPS
2023) in their landmark paper "Language Model Tokenizers Introduce Unfairness Between Languages," which
evaluated 17 tokenizers across dozens of languages and found tokenization length differences of up to 15× for
equivalent text.

3. How Multilingual Embeddings Bridge Scripts — Imperfectly
When two words mean the same thing in different scripts, how does a model know they're related?
Multilingual embedding models create shared vector spaces where semantically similar text in different scripts
clusters together. The mechanisms enabling this are deceptively simple: shared vocabulary and shared
parameters force models to learn partially language-neutral representations. When Hindi and English "computer"
appear in similar contexts across training data, the model converges on similar internal representations despite
entirely different scripts.
Figure 4: Visualization of multilingual embedding space. High-resource languages like English spread across a wide semantic region, while
 low-resource languages like Kannada cluster narrowly — indicating less expressive representations.
 
XLM-RoBERTa (XLM-R) represented the first major leap, training on 2.5 TB of Common Crawl data across 100
languages with a 250K SentencePiece vocabulary. It improved over mBERT by +14.6% on XNLI and +11.4% for
Urdu specifically. More recent models have pushed further: multilingual E5 (mE5-large-instruct), built on XLM-R,
emerged as the best publicly available model on MMTEB (2025), a benchmark spanning 500+ tasks across 250+
languages. BGE-M3, supporting 8,192 token inputs and 100+ languages, pioneered multi-functional retrieval (dense,
sparse, and multi-vector) in a single architecture.
The fundamental tension is the curse of multilinguality: as more languages are added to a fixed-capacity model,
per-language quality degrades. XLM-R experiments show XNLI accuracy drops from 71.8% with 7 languages to
67.7% with 100 languages. The emerging solution is modular architectures — Mixture-of-Experts models like Nomic
Embed v2 (2025) dynamically route inputs to language-appropriate parameter subsets, and adapter-based
approaches like X-MOD add language-specific modules that can be extended without degrading existing languages.

4. Transformers Are Script-Agnostic by Design, but Not
Script-Fair
The transformer doesn't care about script direction. But that doesn't mean all scripts get equal treatment.
A common misconception is that transformers need architectural modifications for right-to-left scripts like Arabic and
Urdu. They do not. Self-attention computes pairwise relationships between all tokens regardless of position, and
even Arabic/Urdu text is stored in left-to-right logical order in Unicode — right-to-left display is purely a rendering
concern. Standard positional encodings (sinusoidal or RoPE) simply mark each token's position numerically.
Figure 5: How tokenization inequality cascades through every transformer layer. More tokens means more positional slots consumed,
 quadratically higher attention cost, and less effective context for generation.
 
That said, research by Baniata et al. (2024) demonstrated that Reverse Positional Encoding — flipping the
standard position assignments — improves performance on Arabic text classification. The real architectural challenge
for Arabic isn't directionality but morphology: Arabic uses non-concatenative morphological templates where tense is
marked by vowel changes inside consonant roots. BPE's sequential merging cannot capture these interleaved
patterns.
The deeper architectural issue is that high token fertility consumes positional encoding slots
disproportionately. For a model with 128K context, Hindi text at 3× fertility gets only ~42,000 word-equivalents of
context compared to ~128,000 for English. This isn't a bug in the attention mechanism — it's a downstream
consequence of tokenization inequality propagating through every layer of the architecture.

5. Training Data Starvation and Compounding Challenges
The root cause of multilingual inequality isn't algorithmic — it's data.
Figure 6: Language distribution in Common Crawl (left) and major LLM training corpora (right). English dominates at 42–95%, while
 Kannada represents just 0.019% of Common Crawl.
 
GPT-3 used 92.65% English training tokens. Llama 3 used approximately 95% English and code. Common Crawl,
the backbone of most training corpora, contains 42.6% English but only 0.21% Hindi and a mere 0.019% Kannada.
This creates a vicious cycle: languages with more web presence get better models, attracting more users who
generate more content.
Script complexity interacts destructively with BPE tokenization. Kannada's abugida script permits up to 18,511
distinct character combinations from 49 base phonemic characters. Conjunct consonants (ottaksharas) combine via
virama characters into single visual glyphs that span 3–6 Unicode code points each. Dependent vowel signs appear
in various positions — above, below, to the left, or to the right of consonant clusters — meaning what humans
perceive as a single orthographic unit can span code points scattered across the sequence.
Morphological richness explodes vocabulary size for agglutinative languages. A single Turkish verb can take
thousands of surface forms. Research evaluating morphological compositional generalization (2024) found that as
morphological complexity increases, LLM performance drops sharply to near zero, while human performance remains
unaffected — suggesting a fundamental limitation rather than a scaling issue.
Code-switching — mixing languages within a sentence, as in Hinglish (Hindi-English) — remains an area where
LLMs significantly underperform fine-tuned smaller models. Zhang et al. (EMNLP 2023) demonstrated that even
GPT-4 loses to task-specific fine-tuned models on code-switched sentiment analysis. The English pivot
phenomenon compounds this further: research by Wendler et al. (2024) showed that decoder-only LLMs internally
convert non-English inputs into English-like latent representations for reasoning, then generate in the target language
— creating an information bottleneck where nuances that don't map cleanly to English concepts are lost.

6. Model Performance Comparison: Who Handles Non-English
Best?
Benchmarking multilingual LLMs has matured dramatically since 2023, with purpose-built evaluations replacing adapted
English benchmarks.
Figure 7: LLM accuracy across English, Hindi, and Kannada. Even the best models show 25–33 percentage point drops from English to
 Kannada on knowledge-intensive tasks.
 
The Artificial Analysis Multilingual Index (2025), testing across 16 languages, places Google Gemini at the top in 13
of 16 languages. For Indic languages specifically, GPT-4o achieves the highest scores on MILU (Multi-task Indic
Language Understanding, AI4Bharat/NAACL 2025) at 72–74% average across 11 Indic languages and 85K
questions, and on IndicMMLU-Pro at 44.8% for Hindi and 39.0% for Kannada.
Specialized Indic models dramatically underperform general-purpose LLMs — that is the most striking finding on
reasoning and knowledge tasks. On IndicMMLU-Pro, models like IndicBERT and MuRIL score near random baseline
levels (~11%). The exception is translation, where specialized models like IndicTrans2 convincingly outperform
general LLMs.
Performance gaps between English and non-English remain substantial: 15–30% absolute accuracy drops from
English to Hindi/Kannada on knowledge-intensive tasks, reaching 38 percentage points for low-resource languages
on MMLU-ProX (2025). STEM subjects show the smallest gaps (most transferable), while culturally specific
knowledge shows the largest — up to 25–35% worse than STEM performance.

7. The Gemini Moment: When an LLM Wrote a Kannada
Anchoring Speech
This entire research journey began with a single prompt to Gemini 3.
When KKNC (Kannada Koota Northern California) invited me to anchor their Sankranti celebration, I needed to craft a
welcome speech that connected the cultural significance of Sankranti with the community's identity — in Kannada.
Having anchored Kannada Rajyotsava the previous semester, I knew the flow I wanted. But for the segue between
welcoming the audience and connecting KKNC's mission to Sankranti, I turned to Gemini 3.
The response was extraordinary. Gemini produced a Kannada anchoring script that wove cultural idioms like
"Ellu-Bella" (the traditional sesame-jaggery exchange), references to village festivals, and the metaphor of Sankranti
as a "home away from home" — all in grammatically coherent Kannada with appropriate honorifics and emotional

8. The Frontier of Solutions: From MAGNET to Sovereign Indic
Models
The gap is real, but it's closing. Here's the research and engineering making it happen.
Figure 8: Three pillars of the multilingual solutions landscape — tokenization innovation, training data expansion, and India's sovereign AI
 ecosystem.
 
MAGNET (NeurIPS 2024) replaces fixed tokenizers with language-script-specific boundary predictors that
dynamically segment byte sequences, achieving 3× token reduction for Indic languages without affecting Latin-script
performance. IndicSuperTokenizer (2025) combines subword and multi-word tokenization with language-specific
pre-tokenization, delivering 44% improved inference throughput over Llama-4 for Indic languages — its key insight
being that careful pre-tokenization outweighs naive vocabulary scaling.
Vocabulary expansion has emerged as the most practical adaptation technique. EEVE (2024) demonstrated that
parameter freezing with subword initialization can boost non-English proficiency within just 2 billion tokens of target
language data. Trans-Tokenization (2024) enables zero-shot cross-lingual vocabulary transfer by initializing new
token embeddings as weighted averages of aligned high-resource embeddings.
India has become a global hub for non-English AI development. AI4Bharat (IIT Madras) produced IndicTrans2, the
first open-source translation model covering all 22 scheduled Indian languages, trained on 230 million bitext pairs.
Sarvam AI (funded at $41M) built Sarvam-1, a 2B-parameter model optimized for 10 Indian languages. Krutrim (by
Ola, $280M funding) launched models natively supporting 22 Indian languages with custom tokenizers. Meta's
NLLB-200 covers 200 languages with a single MoE model, achieving 44% improvement over previous
state-of-the-art.

Conclusion: Tokenization Is the Original Sin, but Solutions Are
Accelerating
The multilingual deficit in LLMs is not a single problem but a cascading failure that begins at tokenization and
amplifies through every subsequent processing stage. A Kannada character that should be one token becomes three
to five byte fragments, consuming context window capacity, inflating costs, and degrading output quality before the
model's actual intelligence even engages.
The most actionable insight from this research is that general-purpose frontier models (GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5)
dramatically outperform specialized Indic models on reasoning and knowledge tasks, while specialized models
dominate only for translation. This suggests the path forward is not building separate models per language but
improving how universal models handle non-English input — through better tokenizers, expanded vocabularies, and
more balanced training data.
The explosive growth of India's AI ecosystem and government-backed initiatives signal that the resources and will to
close this gap now exist. The technical solutions — adaptive tokenization, vocabulary expansion, cross-lingual
transfer learning — are proven. What remains is the engineering and data-curation effort to deploy them at scale.
